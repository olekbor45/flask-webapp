Incoterm Guide

Introduction

When exporting or importing it is crucial to choose an appropriate incoterm. Incoterms may seem tricky and difficult to understand. However, selecting a correct incoterm is a foundational decision that dictates the responsibilities, costs and risks that are devided between the seller and the buyer.

Category E

The buyer bears the responsibility within category E (EXW). The sole responsibility of the seller is to ensure that the products arrive at the agreed delivery address.

Category F

In category F, the method of delivery and handling agreed with the customer in advance is the responsibility of the seller. After that, the buyer takes over the responsibility..

Category C

The seller is responsible for all costs until the goods have arrived at an agreed destination, at category C. When the goods are subsequently transported, the buyer bears the risk.

Category D

All obligations within category D are dependent on the destination(s).

EX-WORKS

Ex Works (EXW) is one of the Incoterms (International Commercial Terms) used in international trade. Under EXW, the seller makes the goods available at their premises or another named place (factory, warehouse, etc.), and the buyer is responsible for all costs and risks involved in transporting the goods from that location to the final destination.

Key Points:

1.Seller's responsibility ends once the goods are made available.

2.Buyer handles everything else: loading, transport, export/import clearance, insurance, etc

3.It gives the least obligation to the seller.

It's commonly used when the buyer has more logistics capabilities or prefers to manage the shipping process themselves.

FREE CARRIER

Free Carrier (FCA) is an Incoterm where the seller delivers the goods to a carrier or another party nominated by the buyer at a named place (often a terminal, port, or transport hub). Once delivered, the risk and cost transfer to the buyer.

Key Points:

1.Seller is responsible for export clearance and delivery to the agreed handover point.

2.Buyer handles transport from that point onward, including insurance and import duties.

3.Can be used for any mode of transport.

4.Offers more flexibility than EXW and is often preferred for containerized shipping.

CARRIAGE PAID TO

Carriage Paid To (CPT) is an Incoterm where the seller arranges and pays for the transport of goods to a named destination, but risk transfers to the buyer once the goods are handed over to the first carrier.

Key Points:

1.Seller pays for main transport to the agreed destination.

2.Buyer assumes risk once goods are handed to the carrier.

3.Seller is also responsible for export clearance.

4.Import duties, taxes, and insurance are buyer’s responsibility.

It’s useful when the seller has better access to shipping arrangements but the buyer wants to manage risk or insurance themselves.

CARRIAGE AND INSURANCE PAID TO

Carriage and Insurance Paid To (CIP) is an Incoterm where the seller delivers the goods to a carrier, pays for the transport to a named destination, and also pays for insurance covering the buyer’s risk during transit.

Key Points:

1.Seller covers transport and minimum insurance to the named destination.

2.Risk transfers to buyer when goods are handed over to the first carrier.

3.Seller handles export clearance.

4.Buyer is responsible for import duties and additional insurance if needed.

CIP is similar to CPT but adds the seller’s obligation to insure the goods.

DELIVERED AT PLACE

Delivered at Place (DAP) is an Incoterm where the seller delivers the goods to a named destination, ready for unloading, and the buyer takes on the risk and cost from that point onward, including import duties and taxes.

Key Points:

1.Seller handles all transport and risk up to the named place of delivery (usually buyer’s location or a terminal).

2.Risk transfers to buyer upon arrival, before unloading.

3.Seller takes care of export clearance, but buyer is responsible for import clearance and duties.

4.Suitable for most transport modes.

DELIVERED AT PLACE UNLOADED

Delivered at Place Unloaded (DPU) is an Incoterm where the seller delivers the goods, unloaded, at a named place of destination. It’s the only Incoterm that requires the seller to unload the goods at the destination.

Key Points:

1.Seller handles transport and unloading at the named place.

2.Risk transfers to buyer after unloading.

3.Seller clears goods for export, but buyer handles import clearance and duties.

4.Suitable for deliveries to terminals, warehouses, or specific sites where unloading is needed.

DELIVERED DUTY PAID

Delivered Duty Paid (DDP) is the Incoterm with the maximum responsibility on the seller, who delivers the goods to a named destination fully cleared for import, with all duties and taxes paid.

Key Points:

1.Seller handles everything: transport, export and import clearance, and all duties/taxes.

2.Risk transfers to buyer only when goods are delivered, ready for unloading.

3.Buyer just receives the goods—minimal involvement.

4.Ideal for buyers wanting a simple, hassle-free delivery.

FREE ALONGSIDE SHIP

Free Alongside Ship (FAS) is an Incoterm used exclusively for sea or inland waterway transport, where the seller delivers the goods alongside the vessel at a named port (e.g., on a quay or barge), and the buyer takes over from that point.

Key Points:

1.Seller is responsible for export clearance and delivery up to the ship’s side at the port.

2.Risk and cost transfer to the buyer once goods are placed alongside the vessel.

3.Buyer handles loading onto the ship, main transport, insurance, and all import formalities.

4.Commonly used for bulk or non-containerized cargo.

FREE ON BOARD

Free on Board (FOB) is an Incoterm used for sea or inland waterway transport, where the seller delivers the goods on board the vessel at the named port of shipment. Once the goods are on board, risk and cost transfer to the buyer.

Key Points:

1.Seller handles export clearance and loading onto the ship.

2.Buyer takes over once goods are on board, covering main transport, insurance, and import formalities.

3.Suitable for bulk cargo and conventional shipping, not containerized freight (for containers, FCA is preferred).

COST AND FREIGHT

Cost and Freight (CFR) is an Incoterm used for sea or inland waterway transport, where the seller pays for the transport of goods to the destination port, but the risk transfers to the buyer once the goods are on board the vessel.

Key Points:

1.Seller covers export clearance and freight costs to the destination port.

2.Buyer takes on risk once goods are loaded onto the ship.

3.Buyer handles unloading, import clearance, duties, and insurance.

4.Like FOB, but with the seller also paying for the freight.

COST, INSURANCE AND FREIGHT

Cost, Insurance and Freight (CIF) is an Incoterm used for sea or inland waterway transport, where the seller delivers goods on board the vessel, pays for the transport and minimum insurance to the destination port, but risk transfers to the buyer once the goods are on board.

Key Points:

1.Seller covers export clearance, freight, and insurance.

2.Buyer assumes risk from the moment goods are loaded on the ship.

3.Buyer handles unloading, import duties, and any extra insurance.

4.Like CFR, but with the added obligation for the seller to insure the goods.
